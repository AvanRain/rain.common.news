Firms’ reported earnings and accruals

 

Abstract

Firms’ reported earnings often differ from their actual cash flows. This difference is referred to as “accruals” and represents the component of earnings that are not yet received in cash. Because of estimation errors, the amount of accruals does not necessarily equal to the amount of cash received in the future, causing high accruals in current period relating to low earnings in subsequent periods. This effect is called the low earnings persistence of accruals.

Example

In 2019, company A reports earnings of $100 million (i.e., operating income after depreciation). The components of cash flows and accruals are $80 million and 20 $million, respectively. Company B reports earnings of $100 million. The components of cash flows and accruals are $30 million and $70 million, respectively.

Based on the idea of low earnings persistence of accruals, holding all other constant, A is expected to have lower earnings than B in 2020.

 

 

 

 

What're accruals?

Earnings reported in firms’ financial reports are often different from their underlying cash flows. This difference is called “accruals”. Large positive accruals indicate that earnings are much higher than cash flows. Earnings and cash flow differ because accounting conventions with respect to the timing and magnitude of revenues and expenses (the so-called revenue recognition and matching principles) are not necessarily based on actual cash inflows and outflows. For example, credits sales are counted toward earnings in the current period, even though they have not yet been received in cash. Similarly, depreciation is deducted from revenues, even though it entails no cash outlays.

 

Why accruals?

Accruals accounting is used to allow financial effects of transactions and events to be recognized when they become probable rather than when the cash consequences are realized 1 . A positive accrual is to anticipate probable future economic benefits (e.g., cash inflows), and a negative accrual is to anticipate probable future obligations (e.g., cash outflows). For example, when products are sold on credits, a positive accrual is originated (an increase in receivables), which is to anticipate the cash inflow when the account receivables are collected. When materials are purchased from suppliers on credits, a negative accrual is originated (an increase in payables), which is to anticipate the cash outflow when the suppliers are paid.

 

Accruals and cash flow realization

Accruals reverse when the cash flows they anticipate are realized. For example, when account receivables are collected, receivables account declines and the corresponding positive accrual reverses to zero. Similarly, the corresponding negative accrual reverses to zero when accounts payables are paid off. As such, at individual transaction level, all accruals will ultimately reverse. However, accrual reversal does not necessarily mean cash flow realization 1,2 .For example, if inventory becomes obsolete, it must be written down. Similarly, when there is evidence indicating the receivables are fraudulent and are unlikely to collect, they also must be written off. In such cases, the original accruals reverse without an accompanying benefit or cash inflow, resulting in ‘accrual estimation errors’.

Accrual estimation errors may arise due to financial reporting manipulation 3 . It is made intentionally and is an ex-ante characterization based on whether the accruals are estimated faithfully and appropriately. For example, a firm increases accounts receivable as its managers record sales prematurely. These accounts receivable could be fraudulent and cannot be collected or collected in an amount much lower than the initial estimate. Or, a firm may decrease current liabilities as its managers understate liabilities such as warranty expenses. Eventually, the firm need to pay more than the liabilities it initially estimates. An accrual manipulation can violate the generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP). For example, to boost current earnings, firms may choose to capitalize overhead costs and exclude them to value the production of inventory, which leads to large inventory errors and the mismatch between accrual change and cash flow realization. .

Accrual errors may also be made unintentionally 1 , which is an ex-post characterization of an accrual based on the difference between the accrual and the subsequently realized benefit. For example, at a point in time, the market demand unexpectedly declines and inventories have to be sold at a lower price or even obsoleted. In this case, measurement errors arise without any accrual manipulation. Alternatively, GAAP that requires firms to carry assets at amounts differing from their expected values will also result in accrual estimation error without accrual manipulation.

Overall, accruals represent managers’ estimation of future benefits or the realization of cash flows and reverse when either (i) the expected future benefits are realized or (ii) there is evidence indicating the future benefits are unlikely to realize. The first case means accruals correctly anticipate future benefits. The second case means accrual estimation error (i.e., accruals are written-off and reverse without benefit realization), which could be caused by either unintentional estimation error or reporting manipulation and distortion.

 

Low earnings persistence of accruals

The extent to which the anticipated future benefits as measured by accruals can be realized in terms of actual cash flows is referred to the earnings persistence of accruals 4 . The accrual component of earnings typically incorporates estimates of future cash flows, deferrals of past cash flows, allocations and valuations, all of which involve higher subjectivity than simply measuring periodic cash flows. As such, accruals are less persistent than cash flow 4,5 . In other words, the current level of accruals has a weaker power than the current level of cash flows in predicting the subsequent earnings.

Suppose there are two firms, A and B. They have the same level of reported earnings. The earnings of firm A contain a higher level of accruals than firm B. Low persistence of accruals means that A is expected to have a lower level of earnings or profitability than B in the coming years.

Accruals have low earnings persistence because of the accrual measurement errors as discussed above 1 . That is, higher accruals are likely to be associated with lower subsequent earnings because the future benefits they anticipate are not fully realized. Accruals are more likely to be subject to measurement errors when they have low reliability 6 . Accrual reliability is affected by the following factors:

  1. Information verifiability. Is the information used in asset and transaction evaluation reliable and does it represent what it purports to represent?
  2. Managerial faithfulness. Do managers have an incentive to be aggressive in applying accounting rules to boost their firms’ current profits for certain personal purposes, such as, career prospects and compensation?
  3. The subjectivity of assets and transactions. Does the valuation of assets and transactions involve large subjectivity, which gives managers more discretion and thus the opportunities to manipulate earnings?

When the valuation of assets and transactions involve great subjectivity and the information used is hard to verify, there could be large error in the estimation of future benefits and obligations, leading to accrual measurement error and the mismatch between accrual reversal and cash flow realization. This problem would be intensified by managerial unfaithfulness 3 . Managers have much leeway with respect to the timing and measurement of revenues and expenses. They also have discretion with regard to the decisions of special items such as restructuring charges and write-offs. To achieve career and other personal objectives, managers may manipulate or distort financial reporting, leading to high earnings and accruals in current period and low performance in subsequent periods.

Other reasons for low future profitability following high accruals

In the absence of accrual estimation errors, high accruals are still likely to relate to low subsequent profitability for the reason of diminishing marginal returns on investments 7 . Firms are likely to invest on the most profitable projects first and the less profitable investments later, leading to the decline in return as more investments are made.

Non-current accruals increase as firms make new capital investments. New capital investments will depress subsequent profitability (usually measured by ROA, earnings/total assets) because new capital investments increase total assets and thereby inflating the denominator of ROA. As a result, even if accruals have no impact on subsequent earnings (i.e., the numerator of ROA), ROA will still decline. If new capital is spent on less favorable investment opportunities, ROA will decline more.

Even if accruals are estimated perfectly and the scale of the firm is fixed, product market shocks might cause accruals negatively associated with subsequent earnings and profitability 8 . For example, an increased demand for products in an industry will increase firms’ profits and working capital (and thus accruals). High profits will attract more new firms entering the industry, causing an increase in competition. At the same time, the input prices will also increase in response to the increased competition, leading to higher inventory costs today but lower future profits when the inventory is sold. Eventually, earnings and profitability are driven back to their long-term equilibrium levels. As a result, accruals are positively associated with current profits but negatively associated with subsequent profits.

 

Summary

Accruals are the difference between firms’ reported earnings and the underlying cash flows and represent firms’ estimates of future benefits and obligations. The accrual component of earnings is less persistent than the cash flow component of earnings in predicting the firms’ future earnings and profitability. This is because accrual estimate involves large subjectivity and are susceptible to managerial manipulation. These biases lead to accrual measurement errors and the lack of realization of benefits as anticipated by the initial accruals. Moreover, even if there are no measurement errors, high accruals could also associate with lower subsequent profitability because of diminishing marginal returns on investment and product market shocks.
